# Validation Report

**Seed:** A metal can become superconducting only if its normal-state charge carriers are holes
(carriers near the top of a nearly-filled electronic band, with a positive Hall coefficient);
superconductivity is impossible in a metal whose normal-state carriers are electrons. The
mechanism: the superconductor-to-normal transition in a magnetic field is reversible,
conserving both mechanical momentum and entropy, so the supercurrent's mechanical momentum
must be transferred to the lattice without dissipation — and only hole carriers, which exchange
momentum with the lattice through the electron-ion interaction near the top of the band, can do
this, while electron carriers near the bottom of the band cannot. This predicts that the
normal-state Hall coefficient of every superconductor is positive (hole-like), and is refuted
by any superconductor whose normal-state carriers are electrons.

## Completed Candidate
**Completion:** `completed_candidate`

A universal sign rule for metallic superconductivity proposing that a positive normal-state Hall coefficient (hole-like carriers) is a strict necessary condition for superconductivity in any ambient-pressure elemental metal. The proposal frames superconductivity as exclusively a hole-pairing instability rooted in Hirsch's kinetic-energy-undressing formalism: carriers near the top of a filled band (hole-like dispersion, m* < 0) can gain kinetic energy by "undressing" from their phonon/electron cloud and delocalizing upon pairing, while electron-like carriers near a band minimum (m* > 0) cannot recoup the kinetic-energy cost of localization, leaving their Cooper channel repulsive or insufficiently attractive. The Hall coefficient sign serves as a macroscopic binary proxy for the microscopic pairing-relevant carrier type, making the rule falsifiable. The research program requires: (i) compiling a complete Hall-coefficient catalog for all known ambient-pressure superconducting elemental metals under a uniform low-temperature, low-field protocol extrapolated to Tc from above; (ii) resolving the aluminum counterexample—either by demonstrating that Al's R_H is positive in the correct regime (possible given its known field-dependent Hall sign) or by reclassifying its pairing-relevant band as hole-like; (iii) deriving within the undressing formalism a gap equation for a generic electron-like band showing the Cooper channel remains strictly repulsive even under strong electron-phonon coupling; (iv) establishing conditions under which R_H sign rigorously coincides with the sign of the pairing-relevant effective mass in multi-band metals; and (v) verifying compatibility with Homes' law and other empirical scaling relations.

**Mechanism:** (1) The normal-state Hall coefficient sign reflects the dominant carrier type set by Fermi-surface curvature and band filling: positive R_H = holes (m* < 0), negative R_H = electrons (m* > 0), in the single-band or uncompensated limit. (2) In the kinetic-energy-undressing picture, superconductivity originates from pairing of hole-like carriers that gain kinetic energy by undressing from their phonon/electron cloud and delocalizing; this instability is favorable only when carriers sit near the top of a filled band, where hole dispersion permits the kinetic-energy gain upon pair formation. (3) For electron-like carriers near a band minimum, the band curvature is reversed: the kinetic-energy cost of localization cannot be recouped by pairing, and the effective interaction projected onto the Cooper channel remains repulsive or insufficiently attractive even when electron-phonon coupling is strong. (4) The undressing pairing instability therefore requires m* < 0 (hole-like dispersion), and since R_H > 0 proxies for m* < 0 in the relevant regime, a positive normal-state Hall coefficient becomes a strict necessary condition for metallic superconductivity. (5) The mechanism predicts that no amount of electron-phonon coupling strength can induce superconductivity in a purely electron-like band, because the topological constraint on kinetic-energy gain is independent of interaction strength.
**Assumptions:** single universal pairing mechanism (kinetic-energy undressing) for all metallic superconductors, Hall coefficient dominated by a single majority carrier type faithfully reflecting Fermi-surface topology in the relevant regime, normal-state low-field Hall sign extrapolates to the pairing-relevant regime above Tc, field- and temperature-dependent sign changes in Hall data do not undermine the classifier once the correct limiting regime is identified, no genuine electron-like-Hall superconducting ambient-pressure elemental metal exists, aluminum's reported negative R_H is either a regime artifact or its pairing-relevant band is hole-like, R_H sign rigorously coincides with the sign of the pairing-relevant effective mass in multi-band superconducting metals, the undressing formalism can be shown to be gauge-invariant and charge-conserving, the Cooper channel is strictly repulsive for electron-like bands even under strong electron-phonon coupling, compatibility with Homes' law and other empirical scaling relations
**Weakest link:** C3/C4 — the universal empirical claim that every ambient-pressure superconducting elemental metal has a positive Hall coefficient and none with a negative coefficient superconducts; aluminum (Tc ≈ 1.2 K, frequently reported negative R_H) is the most direct and potentially fatal counterexample, followed by transition metals with strongly field-dependent Hall signs and pressure-induced superconductors (Li, Ca, Cs) whose carrier type under pressure is uncertain; additionally, no first-principles derivation within the undressing formalism has been published showing that the Cooper channel is strictly repulsive for electron-like bands under any interaction strength, and the mapping between R_H sign and pairing-relevant effective mass sign is not rigorously established for multi-band metals.

## Convincing Case
**Short version:** The proposal asserts a strict binary rule: a metal can superconduct only if its normal-state Hall coefficient is positive (hole-like carriers). This frames superconductivity as an exclusively hole-pairing instability rooted in Hirsch's kinetic-energy-undressing picture, making a falsifiable universal claim that no electron-like metal superconducts. While many canonical superconductors are hole-like, the universal claim faces immediate jeopardy from aluminum (which superconducts but is often reported with a negative Hall coefficient). No rigorous derivation currently shows that electron-like bands cannot support superconductivity, leaving this as a legitimate conjecture under stress rather than an established rule.
**Technical version:** The completed idea proposes that the sign of the normal-state Hall coefficient (R_H) serves as a strict necessary condition for metallic superconductivity: R_H > 0 is required, and R_H < 0 precludes the instability. The mechanistic basis is Hirsch's kinetic-energy-undressing formalism, where pairing is favorable only for carriers near the top of a filled band (hole-like dispersion) because the kinetic-energy gain from delocalization can overcome the cost of localization. For electron-like carriers, reversed curvature allegedly prevents this gain, leaving the Cooper channel repulsive. However, the most serious empirical threat is aluminum (Tc ≈ 1.2 K, frequently reported R_H < 0). No first-principles derivation within the undressing formalism establishes that the Cooper channel is strictly repulsive for electron-like bands under strong electron-phonon coupling, and standard BCS theory successfully treats Al as an electron-phonon superconductor with electron-like curvature.
**Why existing theory leaves room:** Standard BCS theory does not impose a Hall-sign restriction on superconductivity; it permits Cooper pairing for any Fermi-surface curvature given a sufficiently attractive effective interaction, meaning it does not explicitly forbid a universal sign rule—it simply does not predict one. Hirsch's kinetic-energy-undressing theory provides a non-mainstream but internally developed framework in which hole-like dispersion is mechanistically privileged. The empirical observation that many canonical superconductors (Nb, Pb, V) are hole-like provides a partial correlation that conventional BCS does not explain, and Hall-coefficient data for superconducting metals have not been systematically compiled under a uniform protocol, leaving the universal empirical claim untested at the catalog level.
**Why plausible:** The proposal is plausible as a falsifiable conjecture because a significant subset of well-known superconductors do exhibit positive Hall coefficients, providing a positive correlation that invites generalization. The undressing mechanism offers a physically coherent rationale for why hole-like dispersion might be necessary. It remains unvalidated because the universal empirical claim has not been verified against a complete catalog of superconducting metals; aluminum remains an unresolved, potentially fatal counterexample; no derivation within the undressing formalism establishes that electron-like bands cannot support pairing under any interaction strength; and the mapping between R_H sign and the sign of the pairing-relevant effective mass is not rigorously established for multi-band metals.
**Skeptic tests:** Demonstrate that aluminum's normal-state Hall coefficient is positive in the low-temperature low-field regime relevant to pairing above Tc, Compile a complete Hall-coefficient catalog for all ambient-pressure superconducting elemental metals with a single uniform measurement protocol and verify no negative-R_H entry superconducts, Derive within the undressing formalism a gap equation for a generic electron-like band and show rigorously that the Cooper channel remains repulsive even under strong electron-phonon coupling, Derive the conditions under which R_H sign rigorously coincides with the sign of the pairing-relevant effective mass in multi-band metals, Demonstrate compatibility with Homes' law scaling across hole-like and candidate electron-like superconductors
